Carlson et al. provide unprecedented insights into how bacterial genotoxins interact with DNA, specifically examining colibactin's role in DNA interstrand cross-linking. Using advanced mass spectrometry and nuclear magnetic resonance spectroscopy, they revealed the precise molecular interactions between colibactin and DNA. The research discovered that colibactin specifically alkylates within the minor groove of adenine- and thymine-rich DNA regions, explaining the origins of distinctive mutational signatures. This work is crucial for understanding how bacterial products might contribute to cancer development and provides a deeper comprehension of genotoxic mechanisms.

Teloni et al. explored the intricate role of cohesin in maintaining genome stability during DNA repair. Their research revealed how two functionally distinct pools of cohesin collaborate during homologous recombination. Loop-extruding cohesin accumulates across large chromosomal domains to control homology sampling, while cohesive cohesin tethers DNA ends to sister chromatids. This mechanism effectively restricts the space for homology search, highlighting how chromosome conformation contributes to preserving genomic integrity. The study provides fundamental insights into DNA repair mechanisms and could have significant implications for understanding genetic disorders and cancer.
